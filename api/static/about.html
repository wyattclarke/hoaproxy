Why HOAware

Most HOA systems were built for administrative compliance, not resident understanding. Management company portals tend to optimize for liability and operational throughput, which often means fragmented documents, weak search, and limited transparency into how decisions are made. HOAware exists to rebalance that: residents should be able to understand governing documents, evaluate proposals, and participate in neighborhood governance with less friction.

The Problems We See

  • Poor information presentation: key documents are difficult to search, compare, and discuss.
  • Power centralization by proxy: residents often do not get clear visibility into voting dynamics.
  • Low governance innovation: communities inherit old structures without modern participation tools.
  • Decision quality risk: loud minorities can dominate when broad participation is hard.

Our Approach

  • Understand: upload and index governing documents, minutes, and budgets for searchable access.
  • Debate: create shared context for constructive, evidence-linked neighborhood discussion.
  • Evaluate: track whether better information and participation improve community outcomes over time.

Is This Legal?

We are building around resident records-access rights and public-document norms. We also recommend cautious handling of personal data (redaction where appropriate). We are not a law firm, and communities should seek counsel for jurisdiction-specific edge cases.

  • North Carolina planned communities: owner inspection and copying rights in N.C.G.S. 47F-3-118.
  • North Carolina condominiums: owner inspection and copying rights in N.C.G.S. 47C-3-118.
  • Many HOAs are nonprofits: member records rights in N.C.G.S. 55A-16-02 and 55A-16-03.
  • Governing declarations are typically recorded in county land records (planned communities: 47F-2-101, condominiums: 47C-2-101).
  • U.S. copyright law includes fair-use analysis: 17 U.S.C. § 107, and factual/functional material may have limited protection: U.S. Copyright Office Circular 33.
